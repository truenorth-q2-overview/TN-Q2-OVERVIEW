📖 Language Arts

Reading Workshop

Students will delve into impactful historical events that inspired books. By grasping essential facts about historical eras, they aim to comprehend why authors make specific choices regarding inclusion or omission of details in historical fiction. Activities involve filling in backstory to novels, analyzing character responses to challenges, attending to minor characters and missing perspectives, gaining new insights about themes by revisiting story endings, immersing themselves as students of historical eras, reading beyond the text to build background knowledge, distinguishing truth from fiction, analyzing power in conflict, making connections between the past and present, and studying legacies.

The designated book for this unit is The Devil’s Arithmetic by Jane Yolen. In the narrative, during a Passover Seder, 12-year-old Hannah is transported back to 1942 Poland, experiencing the Holocaust horrors as a young Jewish girl named Chaya. Witnessing these atrocities firsthand, Hannah gains a profound understanding of the past and returns with a renewed commitment to remembering and combating hatred.

Writing Workshop

The writing unit revolves around crafting literary essays. Students will undertake the task of researching historical events connected to a book, comparing the past, present, and potential future. The primary goal is to comprehend the current state of society by delving into the past and leveraging book examples to express insightful observations.

Throughout this unit, students will delve into characters’ desires, extract significant ideas from character details, formulate claims, present evidence through storytelling, summarization, and quoting. They will study mentor texts, generate meaningful ideas, write with engagement and precision, troubleshoot common issues in evidence analysis, and undertake comparisons and contrasts of themes across texts.

🔢 Mathematics

Module 2: Ratios and Proportional Relationships

In this module, students learn to reason proportionally by comparing quantities using ratios, unit rates, conversions, and percents. Students move from concrete representations, such as tables and visual models, to more abstract reasoning using equations and proportional strategies. Real-world contexts help students understand how ratios and percents are used in everyday situations, such as shopping, recipes, and measurement.

What Students Will Do

  • Describe and compare ratios using words, tables, and diagrams
  • Use ratio tables and unit rates to solve real-world problems
  • Convert measurements using tables and proportional reasoning
  • Solve percent problems involving part, whole, and percent
  • Explain their thinking using models, tables, and written reasoning

Topic 1: Ratios

Students learn to understand ratios as comparisons between two quantities. They use words, tables, tape diagrams, and real-world examples to describe ratio relationships and compare different situations. (6.RP.1, 6.RP.3A)

Topic 2: Unit Rates and Conversions

Students use unit rates to compare quantities and solve problems. They apply proportional reasoning to convert measurements, including metric conversions, using tables and real-world contexts. (6.RP.2, 6.RP.3B, 6.RP.3D)

Topic 3: Percents

Students build an understanding of percents as ratios out of 100. They solve problems involving finding a percent of a quantity and determining the whole, part, or percent in real-life situations. (6.RP.3C)

🔬 Science

Matter and Energy in Organisms and Ecosystems

The Performance Expectations help students formulate answers to the questions: “How do organisms obtain and use matter and energy? How do matter and energy move through an ecosystem?” Middle school students can use conceptual and physical models to explain the transfer of energy and cycling of matter as they construct explanations for the role of photosynthesis in cycling matter in ecosystems.

They can construct explanations for the cycling of matter in organisms and the interactions of organisms to obtain the matter and energy from the ecosystem to survive and grow. Students have a grade-appropriate understanding and use of the practices of investigations, constructing arguments based on evidence, and oral and written communication. They understand that sustaining life requires substantial energy and matter inputs and the structure and functions of organisms contribute to the capture, transformation, transport, release, and elimination of matter and energy.

Interdependent Relationships in Ecosystems

The Performance Expectations help students formulate an answer to the question, “How do organisms interact with other organisms in the physical environment to obtain matter and energy?” To answer the question, middle school students construct explanations for the interactions in ecosystems and the scientific, economic, political, and social justifications used in making decisions about maintaining biodiversity in ecosystems.

Students can use models, construct evidence-based explanations, and use argumentation from evidence. Students understand that organisms and populations of organisms are dependent on their environmental interactions both with other organisms and with nonliving factors. They also understand the limits of resources influence the growth of organisms and populations, which may result in competition for those limited resources.

Crosscutting Concepts

  • Matter and energy: Tracking flows of matter and energy through ecosystems.
  • Systems and system models: Understanding ecosystems as interconnected systems.
  • Cause and effect: Analyzing how changes in ecosystems affect organisms and populations.

🇨🇷 Español

En este bimestre, la lectura se orienta a reconocer cómo los personajes enfrentan conflictos y toman decisiones que transforman su historia. Con Verano de colores como texto central, los estudiantes fortalecerán la comprensión de estructura narrativa, el análisis de tema e identidad, y la escritura argumentativa y reflexiva. Se integrarán textos no literarios para ampliar perspectivas y sostener opiniones con razones y evidencia.

Lectura (obra central)

Se leerá Verano de colores para analizar desarrollo de personaje, conflicto y tema desde una narrativa cercana a experiencias juveniles. Se trabajará la estructura (inicio–nudo–desenlace), puntos de giro y decisiones de los personajes.

Escritura

Producciones centradas en: resumen con estructura, análisis de personaje (rasgos, motivaciones, cambios), diario/entrada personal desde una voz narrativa y un texto argumentativo breve (opinar con razones y evidencia sobre decisiones del personaje).

Lengua y estilo

Profundización en cohesión: conectores temporales y lógicos; uso de adjetivos y verbos precisos; vocabulario contextual (inferencias).

Ortografía/convenciones

Acentuación (agudas, graves, esdrújulas; hiato si aplica), signos de interrogación/exclamación y corrección de errores frecuentes en borradores.

Textos no literarios integrados

Textos breves sobre identidad, convivencia, toma de decisiones, comunicación asertiva y comparación de perspectivas (entrevista corta, columna juvenil, guía práctica).

Oralidad

Círculos de conversación sobre temas del libro + panel corto por equipos (posturas opuestas con evidencia).

Evaluación (panorama)

Comprobaciones sumativas de lectura, trabajos de análisis (formativos), una tarea escrita extendida (sumativa intermedia) y un producto final (proyecto o presentación) con enfoque en personaje/tema.
